From the public footprint I found, his online image is already pretty strong for a technical builder: personal site, lots of GitHub activity, Microsoft Learn appearances, and visible project work. The biggest opportunity is not “becoming credible,” but making that credibility easier for strangers to understand in 30 seconds.

Here’s where he could improve most:

1. **Sharpen the personal brand into one clear headline**  
   Right now he appears across the web as a Microsoft technical specialist / AI solution engineer / builder of robots, AI agents, and Power Platform solutions. That’s impressive, but slightly broad. A tighter positioning statement like “AI + Power Platform engineer for public sector, with a maker/open-source edge” would make him more memorable.

2. **Make the personal website the unquestioned hub**  
   His site is good, but it would help to make the homepage immediately answer: who he helps, what he’s best at, and what proof backs it up. Adding a prominent “featured work” section with 3–5 strongest case studies would turn a cool portfolio into a stronger professional narrative.

3. **Show outcomes, not just projects**  
   The solutions and projects are interesting, but public-facing case studies become stronger when they spell out business impact: time saved, process improved, users served, cost reduced, deployment scope, or technical complexity.

4. **Consolidate the identity split between “enterprise engineer” and “maker hacker”**  
   He has strong enterprise/public-sector material and also hobbyist/indie engineering work like robotics, quadcopters, F1 analytics, and open-source tools. That combination is actually a strength, but it should be framed as one coherent story instead of two parallel identities. For example: “I build practical AI systems at work and experimental autonomous systems for fun.”

5. **Improve social proof density**  
   His GitHub is active and his Microsoft visibility helps, but adding more testimonials, conference talks, media mentions, awards, or logos of collaborations on the website would make the image feel more established to someone discovering him cold.

6. **Update and standardize links/handles**  
   One search result still points to an older GitHub Pages URL, while the main personal site appears to be on a newer domain. Cleaning up stale links and ensuring every profile points to the same primary site would reduce fragmentation.

7. **Publish more polished thought leadership**  
   A stronger cadence of in-depth essays or technical explainers on AI agents, public sector automation, robotics, or Power Platform architecture would elevate him from “active builder” to “recognized voice.”

8. **Curate GitHub for first impressions**  
   A large number of repositories signals productivity, but newcomers can get lost. Pinning the best repos, making README files visually consistent, and grouping projects by theme would improve discoverability and professionalism.

9. **Own search results more deliberately**  
   Ideally, the first page of results for his name should be dominated by the same clean set: personal site, LinkedIn, GitHub, Microsoft profile/content, and selected articles or talks. Better cross-linking, metadata, and consistent naming would help.

My blunt-but-friendly verdict: **his image looks smart, credible, and genuinely technical already; the main weakness is positioning clarity, not capability.** The internet says “this guy builds cool things,” but with better curation it could more strongly say, “this is a standout expert in AI + public-sector solution engineering with serious maker credibility.”
